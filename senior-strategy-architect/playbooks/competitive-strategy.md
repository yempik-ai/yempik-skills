# Competitive Strategy Playbook

Use for competitor analysis, strategic threats, differentiation, market entry, positioning against alternatives,
category competition, counter-positioning, or “how do we beat X?”

## First diagnosis

Identify the competitive issue:

- Direct competitor is winning on trust, price, distribution, product depth, brand, or ecosystem.
- Status quo is the real competitor.
- Market is commoditizing.
- New entrant changes customer expectations.
- Competitor has stronger channel access.
- Company is copying competitors instead of choosing a different terrain.
- Value chain shift is moving profits away.

## Required strategic choices

- Competitive set: direct competitors, substitutes, status quo, internal workaround.
- Chosen battlefield: segment, use case, channel, geography, buyer type, price tier, service model.
- Advantage: why you can win there.
- Avoided battlefield: where you will not compete.
- Strategic posture: defend, attack, flank, focus, reframe, partner, exit.
- Proof and counter-messaging.

## Useful lenses

- Five Forces: industry profit pressure.
- Value chain: where advantage is created.
- Positioning: parity, difference, reasons to believe.
- Blue Ocean/Four Actions: change value curve.
- Moat mapping: brand, switching costs, network effects, data, cost, distribution, regulatory, capability.
- Win/loss analysis.

## Output must include

- Competitive diagnosis.
- Competitor/status quo map.
- Strength/weakness table.
- Recommended battlefield and posture.
- Differentiation and proof.
- Moves and countermoves.
- Sales/marketing/product implications.
- Metrics and watchlist.

## Strategic postures

| Posture | Use when | Example action |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Competitor is broad and slow | Own a neglected segment/use case |
| Flank | Competitor dominates main category | Win through a channel/workflow they under-serve |
| Reframe | Category criteria favor competitor | Change buying criteria around a different job |
| Premium | Proof and brand support higher value | Add service, guarantees, expertise, outcomes |
| Low-cost | Structural cost advantage exists | Strip features and win price-sensitive segment |
| Partner | Ecosystem access matters | Integrate/co-sell instead of direct fight |
| Defend | Existing base at risk | Improve retention, switching costs, proof, contracts |
| Exit/avoid | Economics are unattractive | Redeploy to better battlefield |

## Metrics

- Win/loss rate by competitor.
- Deal cycle by competitor.
- Price/discount pressure.
- Share of search or branded demand.
- Churn to competitor.
- Feature parity gaps that matter.
- Segment-level market share.
- Sales objection frequency.
- Switching cost signals.

## High-upside moves

- Attack the competitor’s strength where it creates a hidden weakness.
- Make a narrow segment feel “finally built for us.”
- Change the buying checklist with a benchmark, calculator, audit, or new category language.
- Build proof around outcomes competitors claim but do not evidence.
- Create switching support that removes migration risk.

## Failure modes

- Copying the leader.
- Fighting on price without a structural cost advantage.
- Calling features a moat.
- Ignoring substitutes and status quo.
- Differentiating on claims buyers do not value.
