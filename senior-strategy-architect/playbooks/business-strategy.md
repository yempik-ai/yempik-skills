# Business Strategy Playbook

Use for company-level or business-unit direction: business model, market selection, competitive advantage,
capital allocation, operating model, profitability, portfolio, moat, expansion, or turnaround.

## First diagnosis

Find the strategic tension:

- Attractive market but weak advantage.
- Strong product but poor distribution.
- Revenue growth but weak economics.
- Many opportunities but no focus.
- Profit pool shifting across the value chain.
- Capability gap between ambition and execution.
- Competitive pressure eroding differentiation or pricing power.
- Legacy business funding future bets but starving them of focus.

## Required strategic choices

- Where to play: segments, geographies, categories, channels, price tiers, product lines.
- How to win: cost advantage, differentiation, focus, network effects, brand, switching costs, data, distribution, operational excellence.
- Business model: who pays, for what, how often, at what margin, with what cash cycle.
- Capability system: what the company must become excellent at.
- Resource allocation: invest, maintain, harvest, stop.
- Operating cadence: metrics, decision rights, review rhythm.

## Useful lenses

- Strategy choice cascade.
- Five Forces for industry economics.
- Value chain for activity-level advantage.
- Business Model Canvas for model coherence.
- Three Horizons for growth portfolio.
- BCG logic for allocation across products/segments.
- Moat mapping for defensibility.

## Output must include

- Strategic diagnosis.
- Current model and economic logic.
- 2-4 strategic options with trade-offs.
- Recommended strategic choice.
- Where-to-play / how-to-win statement.
- Capability roadmap.
- Capital/resource allocation.
- Metrics and review cadence.
- Risks, assumptions, and validation plan.

## Business model questions

- Is value creation aligned with value capture?
- Is the customer also the payer? If not, who subsidizes whom?
- What is the margin structure and cash conversion cycle?
- What must be true for scale to improve economics?
- What does the business get better at as it grows?
- What customer behavior creates compounding advantage?

## Metrics

Choose by model:

- Revenue growth, gross margin, contribution margin, EBITDA, cash burn, runway.
- CAC, LTV, CAC payback, retention, NRR, repeat purchase.
- Market share by segment, win rate, pricing power.
- Utilization, capacity, throughput, service quality.
- Product adoption, frequency, engagement, activation.
- Partner/channel contribution, supply/demand liquidity for marketplaces.

## High-upside moves

- Narrow the playing field to a segment where the company can win disproportionately.
- Redesign the offer to capture value from the highest-willingness-to-pay use case.
- Build a capability moat around something competitors treat as back-office.
- Exit a distracting segment to free resources for the highest-leverage wedge.
- Convert service delivery or internal workflow into productized IP.

## Failure modes

- Strategy as financial target only.
- “We will be best at everything.”
- No explicit trade-offs.
- Growth that worsens unit economics.
- Copying competitors with better assets.
- Treating a market-size slide as proof of opportunity.
