# Product Strategy Playbook

Use for what to build, roadmap, prioritization, PMF, MVP, product differentiation, user value,
feature bets, activation, and product-led growth.

## First diagnosis

Identify the product problem:

- Problem is not painful enough.
- Target segment is too broad.
- Product does not reach the “aha” moment quickly.
- Users understand value but workflows are too hard.
- Product is useful but not frequent enough.
- Feature requests are masking a positioning or onboarding issue.
- Roadmap is a stakeholder wish list, not a strategy.
- Product creates value but does not capture value.

## Required strategic choices

- User/customer segment.
- Job to Be Done and usage situation.
- Product promise and “aha” moment.
- North Star and input metrics.
- Core product bets and what will not be built.
- Roadmap sequencing.
- Validation plan.

## Useful lenses

- Jobs to Be Done.
- Value Proposition Canvas.
- North Star Framework.
- Build-Measure-Learn.
- RICE/ICE prioritization.
- Opportunity solution tree or opportunity mapping.
- Activation/retention cohorts.

## Output must include

- Product diagnosis.
- Target user and job.
- Product strategy thesis.
- North Star and input metrics.
- Roadmap themes: now/next/later.
- Prioritized bets with rationale.
- Experiments/MVPs.
- Instrumentation.
- Risks and what not to build.

## Product strategy thesis template

“For [target user] who struggles with [job/problem], the product will win by [unique product advantage],
proven through [behavior/metric], starting with [wedge use case], and deliberately not serving [excluded users/use cases] yet.”

## Roadmap rules

- Start with outcomes, not features.
- Separate customer problems from proposed solutions.
- Prioritize the smallest change that tests the biggest assumption.
- Every roadmap item should connect to a strategy pillar or metric.
- Say no to features that dilute the wedge.

## Metrics

- Activation rate and time-to-value.
- Key action completion.
- Usage frequency and depth.
- Retention by cohort.
- Feature adoption and repeat use.
- Customer satisfaction/NPS/qualitative proof.
- Revenue conversion/expansion if monetized.
- Support burden and friction indicators.

## High-upside moves

- Design the product around one “must-have” workflow instead of many nice-to-have features.
- Use onboarding to route users by job, not persona labels.
- Convert repeated manual service into a productized workflow.
- Create a proof dashboard that shows users the value they received.
- Remove or hide features that delay time-to-value.

## Failure modes

- Product strategy as a feature list.
- Roadmap by loudest stakeholder.
- Optimizing new features while activation is broken.
- Using total signups as PMF proof.
- Treating all users as equal when only one segment retains.
