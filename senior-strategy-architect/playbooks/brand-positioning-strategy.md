# Brand and Positioning Strategy Playbook

Use for brand strategy, product positioning, category narrative, differentiation, messaging,
brand identity direction, repositioning, personal brand, or perception change.

## First diagnosis

Identify the positioning problem:

- Category confusion: customers do not know what to compare you with.
- Parity gap: customers doubt you meet category basics.
- Difference gap: they understand you but see no reason to choose you.
- Proof gap: the claim is attractive but not believable.
- Relevance gap: the message is differentiated but not tied to urgent pain.
- Memorability gap: the brand lacks distinctive assets or repeatable language.
- Audience mismatch: the brand speaks to too many people.

## Required positioning choices

- Target customer/audience.
- Frame of reference/category.
- Points of parity: what must be true to be considered.
- Point of difference: the chosen basis of superiority.
- Reasons to believe: proof, credibility, product evidence, founder story, results.
- Enemy/status quo: what the brand is against.
- Brand voice and distinctive assets.

## Positioning statement template

“For [target], [brand/product] is the [frame of reference] that [point of difference], because [reasons to believe].
Unlike [alternative/status quo], we [strategic contrast].”

## Message architecture

Create:

- One-line positioning.
- Core promise.
- 3 message pillars.
- Proof points under each pillar.
- Objection responses.
- Tagline or campaign line if useful.
- “Words we use / words we avoid.”

## Brand narrative structure

1. The old way is broken.
2. The world has changed.
3. The target now needs a new approach.
4. The brand/product embodies that approach.
5. Proof shows it works.
6. The customer is invited into a sharper identity/outcome.

## Metrics

- Qualitative message comprehension.
- Branded search / share of search.
- Direct traffic.
- Aided and unaided awareness.
- Consideration/preference.
- Conversion rate by message.
- Sales objection frequency.
- Distinctive asset recognition.

## High-upside moves

- Choose a polarizing enemy that the target already feels but has not named clearly.
- Reframe the category around the target’s real job, not the product’s features.
- Build a “proof pyramid”: fast proof, deep proof, social proof, technical proof.
- Create distinctive language competitors would be afraid or unable to use.
- Tighten the frame of reference before trying to sound unique.

## Failure modes

- Differentiation with no parity: unique but not trusted.
- Parity with no difference: credible but forgettable.
- Abstract claims with no proof.
- Branding that sounds good internally but does not change customer behavior.
- Copying category leaders while claiming to be different.
