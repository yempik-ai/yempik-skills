# Universal Strategy Method

Use this method for any serious strategy task. The goal is to convert context into hard choices and coherent action.

## 1. Define the strategic job

Before prescribing tactics, identify the real job:

- Create demand.
- Capture existing demand.
- Improve conversion.
- Improve trust/proof.
- Increase retention or expansion.
- Change positioning/perception.
- Enter a market or segment.
- Launch a product.
- Improve unit economics.
- Build a moat.
- Allocate resources.
- Respond to competition.
- Raise capital.
- Build a repeatable operating system.

## 2. Diagnose the binding constraint

Most weak strategies fail because they solve the wrong problem. Choose the dominant constraint:

| Constraint | Signs | Strategic implication |
|---|---|---|
| Market selection | Everyone could be a customer; no urgent segment | Narrow the beachhead |
| Problem intensity | Nice-to-have, long sales cycles, low urgency | Reframe or find higher-pain use case |
| Value proposition | Users do not “get it” quickly | Sharpen promise/proof/category |
| Distribution | Product is good but no repeatable channel | Find channel-market fit |
| Trust | Leads exist but do not convert | Build proof, authority, risk reversal |
| Conversion | Traffic/leads but low close | Fix offer, funnel, sales process |
| Retention | Acquisition works but churn is high | Fix activation/value delivery |
| Monetization | Usage exists but revenue weak | Repackage, price, expand |
| Capability | Strategy needs skills/assets team lacks | Build/buy/partner/simplify |
| Competition | Market crowded and claims are similar | Reposition, flank, specialize |
| Economics | Growth destroys margin/cash | Redesign pricing, CAC, payback, scope |
| Timing | Market not ready or trend fading | Change sequence or wedge |

State the constraint explicitly. Then make every major action address it.

## 3. Build the strategy kernel

Use this structure:

1. Diagnosis: “The core issue is not X; it is Y.”
2. Guiding policy: “Therefore we will win by doing A, not B.”
3. Coherent actions: “The coordinated actions are 1, 2, 3.”

Avoid “do more marketing,” “improve awareness,” “create value,” or “scale” as guiding policies.
They are goals or themes, not strategic choices.

## 4. Make the choice cascade

Answer:

1. Winning aspiration: what does winning mean, specifically?
2. Where to play: segment, market, use case, geography, category, channel.
3. How to win: advantage, wedge, offer, motion, proof, economics.
4. Capabilities required: skills, assets, systems, team, data, partnerships.
5. Management systems: cadence, metrics, experiments, owners, review process.

If any answer is vague, the strategy is not ready.

## 5. Generate strategic alternatives before committing

For important decisions, compare 2-4 options:

| Option | Best if | Upside | Risk | Reject if | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Then choose one. A senior strategy rejects attractive distractions.

## 6. Translate into an action architecture

Actions must fit together. Organize them as:

- Pillar 1: fixes the binding constraint.
- Pillar 2: builds the chosen advantage.
- Pillar 3: creates learning/measurement.
- Pillar 4: improves economics or scalability.

Each pillar needs owner, cadence, metric, and first action.

## 7. Use “proof before scale”

Before recommending scale, identify the proof needed:

- For marketing: repeatable channel economics or credible demand signals.
- For GTM: a segment that converts with repeatable messaging and motion.
- For product: retention, activation, or repeated user value.
- For sales: pipeline conversion, win rate, sales cycle, ACV, payback.
- For pricing: conversion/retention impact and willingness-to-pay evidence.
- For fundraising: narrative, traction, market, and milestone credibility.

## 8. Design the learning loop

Every strategy should produce new information quickly.

- Hypothesis: what must be true?
- Test: what action tests it?
- Metric: what signal proves/disproves it?
- Threshold: what number causes continue/pivot/stop?
- Cadence: when will the decision be reviewed?

## 9. Stress-test the strategy

Ask:

- What would make this fail even if execution is good?
- Which assumption is most fragile?
- What are competitors likely to copy or counter?
- Where could unit economics break?
- What must we explicitly not do?
- Does this strategy create compounding advantage, or just one-off effort?

## 10. Output standard

A final strategy should feel like an operating memo a senior partner would send to a founder or exec:

- clear opinion;
- no hedging without reason;
- useful assumptions;
- practical detail;
- trade-offs;
- first actions;
- metrics;
- creative leverage.
